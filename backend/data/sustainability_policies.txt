# GreenChain Sustainability Policies — Indian Logistics Fleet
# One policy per line. Lines starting with # are comments and are ignored.

# ── Emission Benchmarks ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The India national average for road freight emissions is 0.90 kg CO2 per km; all fleet operations must be tracked against this baseline.
The EU 2030 heavy vehicle CO2 target is 0.55 kg CO2 per km; this is the medium-term fleet average target for GreenChain-managed routes.
Electric vehicle (EV) trucks on Indian roads currently achieve approximately 0.05 kg CO2 per km including grid emission factor; this is the best-in-class benchmark.
Electric rail freight achieves approximately 0.015 kg CO2 per km for Indian Railways electrified corridors; rail is the preferred mode for routes exceeding 600 km where service levels allow.
CNG trucks achieve approximately 0.50–0.55 kg CO2 per km and are the preferred replacement for diesel on short-haul routes under 400 km.
Any shipment recording CO2 per km above 1.0 kg must be automatically escalated for immediate driver review and route assessment.
Fleet-wide CO2 per km must be reported weekly; the monthly reduction target is 2% against the prior month rolling average.

# ── Green Score Grading ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Green Score Grade A (score 90–100) indicates exemplary sustainability performance; vehicle type, driver behaviour, and cargo utilisation are all optimised.
Green Score Grade B (score 75–89) indicates good performance; minor improvements in driver behaviour or load factor could push to Grade A.
Green Score Grade C (score 60–74) indicates acceptable performance; targeted coaching and load consolidation reviews are recommended.
Green Score Grade D (score 45–59) indicates poor performance; a formal corrective action plan must be initiated within 48 hours.
Green Score Grade F (score below 45) indicates critical performance failure; the shipment must be reviewed for immediate intervention and the driver enrolled in coaching.
Green Score is calculated from four weighted factors: CO2 efficiency vs India baseline (40%), driver behaviour score (30%), vehicle type bonus (20%), and cargo load utilisation (10%).
EV trucks receive a +10 point vehicle type bonus to their Green Score; trains receive +8; CNG trucks receive +3; diesel trucks receive 0.

# ── Carbon Credits ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carbon credit calculation follows Gold Standard Verified Emission Reduction (VER) methodology.
One carbon credit equals one tonne (1,000 kg) of CO2 avoided compared to the India average baseline of 0.90 kg per km.
Carbon credits are valued at INR 800 per credit (approximately USD 9.50) for internal reporting and fleet benchmarking.
Only shipments running below the India baseline of 0.90 kg CO2 per km qualify for positive carbon credit generation.
Shipments above the 0.90 kg CO2 per km baseline generate a negative credit impact that offsets gains from cleaner vehicles in the fleet.
A fleet running 80% EV trucks on high-volume corridors can realistically generate INR 50,000 to 100,000 per month in carbon credits.
Carbon credits should be reported monthly to ESG reporting teams with per-shipment breakdowns and route-level aggregation.

# ── Vehicle Transition Policy ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
EV trucks are the preferred vehicle type for all short-haul urban and peri-urban routes under 300 km with established charging infrastructure.
CNG trucks are the recommended diesel replacement for inter-city routes of 300–600 km where EV charging is not yet available.
Diesel trucks should be phased out on any route where EV or CNG alternatives have been validated with equivalent service levels.
For routes exceeding 600 km with high cargo density (above 30 tonnes), rail freight should be evaluated as the primary mode.
Vehicle type upgrades on a lane must be evaluated using the Route Alternatives tool before each contract renewal.
Leasing partners should be notified 90 days in advance when a lane transitions from diesel to EV or CNG.

# ── Driver Behaviour ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Driver behaviour score is calculated from three signals: idling time ratio (33%), harsh braking event count per 100 km (33%), and speed consistency against the posted limit (33%).
Idling for more than 10 consecutive minutes without cargo loading or unloading activity must generate a medium-severity idle alert.
More than 3 harsh braking events per 100 km in a single shift triggers an automatic driver coaching recommendation.
Drivers with a behaviour score below 60 for two consecutive weeks must be enrolled in a formal eco-driving programme.
Speed variance above 25% of the route average sustained for more than 30 minutes must be flagged as an anomaly.
Top-performing drivers (score 90+) should be recognised monthly in fleet communications to incentivise sustainable behaviour.

# ── Route Optimisation ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Route alternatives must be assessed for every shipment recording a Green Score of D or F.
When a route alternative reduces predicted CO2 by more than 20%, the recommendation must be escalated to the fleet manager within 4 hours.
Rail corridor options must be evaluated for all origin-destination pairs with annual freight volume exceeding 500 tonnes.
Load consolidation should be evaluated for any shipment with cargo utilisation below 60% of vehicle capacity.
Empty return legs must be tracked and consolidation opportunities flagged using the Route Alternatives tool within 24 hours of delivery completion.
The Delhi–Mumbai freight corridor has an established rail option (Indian Railways Container Corporation) that reduces CO2 by approximately 97% versus diesel truck.

# ── Alert Thresholds ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High-severity emission spike alerts are triggered when CO2 per km exceeds 1.0 kg on any single segment reading.
Medium-severity delay risk alerts are triggered when ETA slips beyond the original delivery window by more than 30 minutes.
Medium-severity harsh braking alerts are triggered when 5 or more harsh braking events are recorded within a 90-minute window.
Low-severity idle alerts are triggered when cumulative idling exceeds 12 minutes in any 60-minute period.
All high-severity alerts must be acknowledged by a fleet manager within 2 hours of creation.
Unacknowledged high-severity alerts escalate to the sustainability officer after 4 hours.

# ── Reporting and Compliance ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Weekly fleet emission reports must include: total CO2 kg, average CO2 per km, number of Grade A/B/C/D/F shipments, and carbon credits earned.
Monthly ESG reports must include: CO2 reduction percentage vs prior month, cumulative carbon credits, and top 3 emission hotspot routes.
Emission data must be retained for a minimum of 3 years to support GHG Protocol Scope 3 supply chain audits.
All CO2 calculations use the IPCC AR6 emission factor of 2.68 kg CO2 per litre of diesel and 2.10 kg CO2 per litre of CNG equivalent.
EV truck emissions are calculated using the India grid emission factor of 0.82 kg CO2 per kWh multiplied by average energy consumption of 0.62 kWh per km.
